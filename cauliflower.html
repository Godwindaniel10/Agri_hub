Cauliflower

Complete Guide On How To Grow Cauliflower

Cauliflower is a cool-weather crop that requires 55 to 100 days of cool, even temperatures to reach harvest. Plant 1 to 2 plants per household member.

Cauliflower is a cool-weather half-hardy biennial grown as an annual. Cauliflower is grown for its edible buds which form a solid head atop single stalks. Heads are sometimes called curds. Heads can be cream, white, purple, or green colored. Broccoflower is a hybrid cross between cauliflower and broccoli; broccoflower have green heads.

How to Grow Cauliflower

When to Plant Cauliflower

  1. Cauliflower requires 55 to 100 days of cool, even temperatures to reach harvest.
  2. Start cauliflower seed indoors 6 to 10 weeks before the last frost in spring.
  3. Cauliflower germinates at 45°F (7°C). (Cauliflower is usually grown from transplants.)
  4. Transplants can go into the garden 2 to 6 weeks before the last frost, usually 6 weeks after sowing when plants have 4 to 5 true leaves.
  5. Direct seed cauliflower into the garden where the soil temperature is between 65°F and 75°F (18-24°C) and the weather will remain cool.
  6. Crops for a fall harvest can be direct-seeded 8 to 12 weeks before the first expected frost in fall.
  7. Cauliflower does not like extremes of temperature, hot or cold it does not tolerate dry conditions.
  8. Plant cauliflower in autumn for a winter harvest where winters are mild and frost-free.
  9. Extreme temperatures will cause cauliflower to bolt and go to seed.

Where to Plant Cauliflower

  1. Cauliflower grows best in rich, well-drained, moisture-retentive soil with a pH within the 6.5 to 8.0 range.
  2. Plant cauliflower in full sun.
  3. Broccoflower prefers partial shade.
  4. Add aged compost to planting beds before planting.

How to Plant Cauliflower

  1. Sow cauliflower seeds ½ inch deep and 2 to 3 inch (2.5-7.6cm) apart.
  2. Thin plants to 15 to 24 inches (38-61cm) apart space rows 24 to 30 inches (61-76cm) apart.
  3. Set leggy or cooked-stemmed transplants deeply, up to their first leaves, so that they will not grow top-heavy.
  4. For succession crops, plant a couple of heads at a time or plant early and midseason varieties at the same time.

Water Requirements for Cauliflower

  1. Cauliflower requires evenly moist soil for uninterrupted, vigorous growth and head formation.
  2. Do not let the ground dry out.
  3. Add aged compost to the planting bed before planting and as a side dressing again at midseason.

Protecting Against Disease and Pests

  1. Cauliflower can be attacked by cutworms, cabbage loopers (preceded by small yellow and white moths), and imported cabbage worms.
  2. These pests can be controlled by fine mesh row covers, handpicking, and spraying with Bacillus thuringiensis.
  3. Cauliflower is susceptible to root rots–an initial symptom is the yellowing leaves.
  4. Plant disease-resistant varieties, keep the garden free of debris, and avoid handling plants when they are wet.
  5. Remove and destroy infected plants immediately so they cannot spread the disease to healthy plants.
  6. Downy mildew which occurs in cool, wet conditions can cause cauliflower head to brown.

How to Harvest Cauliflower

  1. Time from planting to harvest is 55 to 100 days for cauliflower grown from transplants 85 to 130 days for cauliflower grown from seed.
  2. Under good growing conditions, the heads develop rapidly to about six to eight inches in diameter.
  3. The mature head should be compact, firm, and white. Cut the whole head from the main stem.
  4. The leaves can be cooked like collards or cabbage.

Storing Cauliflower

  1. Cauliflower will keep for one week in the refrigerator unwashed and wrapped in plastic.
  2. Cauliflower can be frozen, pickled.

Asparagus

Complete Guide On How To Grow Asparagus

Asparagus is the first sign of Spring in many vegetable gardens. Stalks or spears begin to break the soil in early spring as soon as the soil temperature reaches 50°F (10°C).

Asparagus is a perennial vegetable that requires three to four years to become established and productive. Once established, an asparagus plant can produce spears for 15 to 20 years or more.

How to Grow Asparagus

When to Plant Asparagus

  1. You can grow asparagus from seeds, crowns (roots), or transplants.
  2. Start seed indoors 12 to 14 weeks before the last frost.
  3. Transplant seedlings or plants into the garden after the last frost.
  4. Plant asparagus seeds or crowns in the garden in early spring as soon as the soil is workable. The soil temperature should be 50°F (10°C) or warmer.
  5. Allow asparagus 3 years to establish its roots before you begin harvesting.

Where to Plant Asparagus

  1. Asparagus is a perennial. Plant asparagus in a spot where plants can grow undisturbed and productive for 15 to 20 years.
  2. Plant asparagus in full sun where it gets plenty of air circulation; this will help prevent disease. Asparagus can tolerate partial shade.
  3. Plant asparagus out of prevailing winds; wind can damage or break stalks.
  4. Asparagus grows best in loose, compost-rich well-drained soil. Sandy loam is good soil for growing asparagus.
  5. Add 3 to 4 inches (7-10cm) of aged compost to the planting bed and turn it under to 12 inches (30cm) deep or more before planting asparagus.
  6. Plant in raised beds if your native soil is poor or constantly wet.
  7. A soil pH between 6.5 and 7.5 is best for growing asparagus.

How to Plant Asparagus

  1. Asparagus plants grown from seed are less susceptible to transplant shock than those started from crowns.
  2. Asparagus plant started from seed will be more productive over the course of its life than a plant started from a crown.
  3. Sow seed 8 to 10 weeks before you plan to set transplants in the garden.
  4. Soak seed in compost tea for 5 to 10 minutes before planting; this will help reduce disease problems.
  5. Sow seed 1½ inch (3.8cm) deep in a seed-starting mix or light potting soil. Sow seeds in individual containers.
  6. Seeds will germinate in 7 to 21 days at 75°F (24°C).
  7. Place seedlings in a cold frame, plastic tunnel, or greenhouse to grow on until outdoor temperatures are warm enough for transplanting.
  8. The optimal growing temperature is 60° to 70°F (15-21°C).
  9. Male plants produce more spears than female plants. You can cull out female plants by looking at the flowers.
  10. Female flowers have three-lobed pistils; male flowers are larger and longer than female flowers.

Water Requirements for Asparagus

  1. Keep asparagus planting beds evenly moist but not wet throughout the growing season.
  2. Mature plants can survive without extra watering, but the stalks may become stringy and woody.

Protecting Against Disease and Pests

  1. Asparagus beetles may attack asparagus, especially in commercial asparagus growing districts.
  2. Asparagus beetles and larvae chew spear in spring and feed on fronds in summer.
  3. Pick off and destroy asparagus beetles or spray with insecticidal soap or pyrethrins.
  4. Asparagus is generally disease-free.
  5. Fungal diseases such as rust and fusarium wilt can attack asparagus. Spray-mist plants with compost tea to prevent fungal diseases.

How to Harvest Asparagus

  1. Begin the asparagus harvest when plants are three years old and fully developed.
  2. Pick spears for only two weeks the third year (which is the first harvest); let plants continue to establish themselves; over-harvesting can weaken plants and cause future harvests to be small.
  3. The fourth year after planting, extend the harvest to 4 weeks.
  4. Every year after the fourth year, you can extend the harvest by a week or two until you are harvesting up to 8 weeks.
  5. Cut spears when they are 6 to 10 inches (15-25cm) tall and at least the diameter of a pencil. Tips should be firm and closed.
  6. Snap spears off at soil level with your fingers at or just below ground level. If you use a knife, be careful not to injure nearby crowns.
  7. When spear bracts begin to feather out, it is too late to eat them.
  8. If temperatures approach 90°F (32°C) at harvest time, harvest daily; heat can cause bracts to open prematurely.

Storing Asparagus

  1. Asparagus is best used fresh.
  2. Asparagus will keep for up to 1 week in the refrigerator. Place spear upright in an inch or so of water and refrigerate.
  3. Freeze asparagus after blanching.

Different Types of Asparagus

  1. ‘Jersey Knight’ (male): heavy producer.
  2. ‘Jersey Giant’ (male): grows well in the East and Midwest.
  3. ‘Larac’ (male/female): pale, nearly white, French cultivar.
  4. ‘Mary Washington’ (female): heirloom traditional variety.
  5. ‘Millennium F1’ (male): grows well in very cold northern gardens.
  6. ‘Purple Passion (male/female): colorful, tender.
  7. ‘UC 157’ (male): a good choice for West Coast weather.
  8. ‘Viking’ (male): excellent for cold climates, tender, tasty.

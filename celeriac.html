Celeriac

Complete Guide On How To Grow Celeriac

Celeriac is a cool-season biennial grown as an annual. It is similar in growth habit and requirements as celery.

Celeriac is grown for its large, swollen root that looks something like a turnip. The root develops at soil level similar to a turnip but celeriac is a member of the celery family and has a similar growth habit. From the root, a rosette of dark green leaves sprout atop hollow stems.

WHERE TO GROW Celeriac

  1. Celeriac grows best in full sun but will tolerate light shade.
  2. Plant celeriac in moisture-retentive but well-drained soil rich in organic matter.
  3. Add aged compost to planting beds before plants and again at midseason.
  4. Celeriac is a heavy feeder; give plants a side dressing of compost tea every 2 to 3 weeks during the growing season.

PLANTING

Celeriac Planting Time

  1. Celeriac grows best in cool weather, especially where nights are cool.
  2. Grow celeriac in spring in cold-winter regions; in warm-winter regions grow celeriac beginning in late summer so that it matures in cool weather.
  3. Celeriac is slow to germinate and is best grown from transplants.
  4. Sow seed indoors as early as 10 weeks before the average last frost date in spring.
  5. Plants can go into the garden on the average date of the last frost.
  6. Celeriac requires up to 120 days to reach harvest.
  7. A late summer crop can be sown directly in the garden where there is enough time for a second harvest.

Celeriac Planting and Spacing

  1. Sow celeriac seed ⅛ inch deep.
  2. Place a seed cloth or burlap over the seeding area to keep the soil moist until plants sprout.
  3. When plants are 3 to 4 inches (7.6-10cm) tall thin them from 6 to 8 inches (15-20cm) apart or transplant them into the garden at that distance.
  4. Space rows 24 to 30 inches (61-76cm) apart.
  5. Celeriac is often set into the garden in 3- to 4-inch (7.6-10cm) deep trenches, similar to celery.
  6. As the plants grow, soil can be mounded around the exposed root.

Container Growing Celeriac

  1. Celeriac can be grown in a container, but not well.
  2. Choose an 8-inch (20cm) container for a single plant.

Watetring and Feeding Celeriac

  1. Celeriac is shallow-rooted and requires regular watering.
  2. Lack of soil moisture will cause celeriac to stop growing.
  3. Keep the top few inches of soil moist at all times.

Caring for Celeriac

  1. Keep celeriac planting beds weed-free to avoid competition for water and nutrients.
  2. Cultivate carefully to avoid celeriac’s shallow roots.
  3. As the root develops, snip off the side roots and hill up the soil over the swollen bulbous root to blanch it.
  4. The outside of the root will blanch white but the flesh will remain a brownish color.

Celeriac Pests and Disease

  1. Celeriac has no serious pest problems.
  2. Celeriac has no serious disease problems.

Harvesting Celeriac

  1. Harvest celeriac when the swollen root is 3 to 4 inches (7.6-10cm) across or slightly larger.
  2. Cut stems close to the knobby root; use a garden fork to lift the roots.
  3. Celeriac will increase with flavor following a light frost but should be harvested before the first hard freeze.
  4. Leaves can be used to flavor soups and stews. From seed, celeriac will reach harvest in 110 to 120 days.

Storing and Preserving Celeriac

  1. Celeriac will keep in the refrigerator up to one week, or store the root in a cold, moist place for 2 to 3 months.
  2. Celeriac will keep in the ground where the soil does not freeze.
  3. Leaves can be used as an herb in soups and stews.

Celeriac Varities to Grow

  1. ‘Alabaster’ (120 days)
  2. ‘Giant Prague’ (120 days).
